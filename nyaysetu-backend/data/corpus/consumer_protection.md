# Consumer Protection in India — Plain-Language Summary

The Consumer Protection Act, 2019 replaced the 1986 Act and created a stronger framework
for consumer rights, including e-commerce.

## Who is a "consumer"?
Anyone who buys goods or hires services for a consideration. The definition explicitly
includes online and tele-shopping transactions.

## Three-tier dispute resolution forums
- **District Commission** — claims up to ₹50 lakh.
- **State Commission** — claims above ₹50 lakh up to ₹2 crore.
- **National Commission (NCDRC)** — claims above ₹2 crore.

A consumer may file a complaint where they reside or work, in addition to where the
opposite party operates. There is no court fee for claims up to ₹5 lakh.

## How to file a complaint
1. Send a written legal notice to the seller/service provider giving them an opportunity
   to remedy the deficiency (usually 15–30 days).
2. If no resolution, file a complaint before the appropriate Commission. Complaints can be
   filed online via the e-Daakhil portal (edaakhil.nic.in).
3. Attach evidence: invoice, payment proof, warranty card, correspondence, photographs of
   defective goods.

## Time limits
A complaint must be filed within two years from the date the cause of action arose. Delay
can be condoned for sufficient cause.

## Remedies the Commission can grant
- Refund of price paid
- Replacement of goods or re-performance of service
- Compensation for loss, including punitive damages in egregious cases
- Removal of defects or deficiencies in service
- Discontinuation of unfair trade practices and misleading advertisements

## Product liability
Under the 2019 Act, a manufacturer, seller, or service provider can be held liable for
harm caused by a defective product. The consumer does not need to prove negligence; defect
plus harm is enough.
