# Filing a First Information Report (FIR) — Procedure under CrPC

## What is an FIR?
An FIR is a written document prepared by the police when they receive information about
the commission of a cognizable offence. It is registered under Section 154 of the Code of
Criminal Procedure, 1973 (CrPC).

## Who can file an FIR?
- The victim of the offence
- A witness or anyone with knowledge of the offence
- The police themselves, on their own information

## Procedure
1. Approach the police station with jurisdiction over the place of occurrence. If unsure,
   you may file a **Zero FIR** at any police station; it will later be transferred.
2. State the facts orally or in writing. If oral, the officer must reduce it to writing
   and read it back to you.
3. Sign the FIR after verifying the contents.
4. Obtain a free copy of the registered FIR — this is your right under Section 154(2).

## What if the police refuse to register an FIR?
Under Section 154(3) CrPC and the Supreme Court's directions in *Lalita Kumari v. State of
U.P.*, registration is mandatory for cognizable offences. Remedies available:
- Send a written complaint to the Superintendent of Police (SP).
- Approach the jurisdictional Magistrate under Section 156(3) CrPC for a direction to
  register the FIR.
- File a private complaint under Section 200 CrPC.

## Information to include
- Date, time, and place of incident
- Names and descriptions of accused (if known)
- Sequence of events in chronological order
- Witnesses, if any
- Any physical evidence, photographs, or documents

## After registration
- The police investigate and submit a report (charge-sheet or closure report) to the
  Magistrate.
- The complainant can apply for a copy of the case diary and follow up on progress.
- For non-cognizable offences, the police record an entry in the daily diary and direct
  the complainant to the Magistrate.
